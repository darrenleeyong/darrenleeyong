Project info and Problem statement

As Arch continues adding more customers / pilots in its pipeline, we have to communicate succinctly what Arch does, and to update available information such as the products and services offered and the value it brings. The marketing website has a few pages but it does not inform which could lead to a potential loss in leads/sales.

Visit the website here.

Tools Used

  • Sketch App
  • Adobe Photoshop
  • Adobe Illustrator
  • Google Analytics
  • InVision App
  • Github
  • Google Docs

Goals and Team

Addressing the problem above, there is a need to update the marketing website with new information and to allow visitors to find that information fast.

Goals for this project would be:

  1. Priority to update the content in a coherent manner to communicate what Arch does and our progress.
  2. Showcase Arch's solutions as well-established, ready, and working, while not overstating what we can do.
  3. Increase customer engagement through strategic UX design to become customers and partners of Arch.

I would be working closely with the business development team — Arch’s CEO and Cofounder, Andrew Scheuermann, and VP of Marketing, Tallis Blalack, for the website expansion project. I will be taking on the role as the lead UX designer to conduct research, design mocks and prototypes, and then develop the new pages. We would take on a pseudo-Agile approach, going lean on the process due to time constraints.

Audience

On a high level, Arch provides end-to-end IoT solutions targeting operational technology (OT) companies — OEMs (Original Equiment Manufacturers) of industrial equipment and operators.

Our target audience would be:

  • New prospects/customers who would like to know find out more about what Arch does the value we provide
  • Existing customers in discussions with, to help them understand more about Arch’s solutions and to establish credibility.
  • The broader community, including our team members, family, public partnerships, and the media, to inform how we are working towards our vision for a more connected and sustainable Earth.

Research and Best Practices

Going into the space required a lot of understanding of the industries. I learned the processes within the relevant industries such as shipping and logistics and outdoor agriculture to gain a better understanding of how we to communicate value with Arch’s products. I then conducted research on our competitors through their websites, observed the layout of their websites, analyzed and drew insights to see what worked and what did not, and brainstormed for ideas for the new pages.

Persona development

Research plays an important role to inform the way we build the pages. As we were taking a lean approach, I instead read on the customers and companies currently engaged to develop a better understanding on their demographics. I then developed personas to fit into the frame of our target audience.

Personas are a great reference to better understand the basic problems and needs of the audience. It provides 'a person'' for the company to build, with the user in mind — for every meeting, every decision, every design, and every line of code. The result is a user-centered product that brings value and is enjoyable to use.

Wireframes

The next step was to get a bird’s eye view of the project as a guide. I did low fidelity sketches moved to a medium-fidelity wireframe of entire sitemap to better communicate my vision of the project and presented it to the business team, allowing me to proceed on with the project. We made the decision that the ‘Solutions’ part of the site was the first portion to be rolled out as it was highly implementable and as a first step be able to show use cases.

Mock ups

Initially, I used Photoshop to design the website for the ’Solutions' branch. It was done before a design system was in place. I attempted to implement some sort of consistency / style and kept the work organized by color-coding the layers and adopting a simple naming convention. Going lean, I did a few high-fidelity mock ups and presented to the business team how these final pages would look like. The page layout and display of the information was given a go-ahead and then I proceeded to work on the rest of the branch.

Halfway through, I picked up design systems and implemented a very basic level of it. I also switched over to Sketch App as my primary tool after picking up speed and proficiency in it. I manage to recreate the entire portion of the ‘Solutions’ pages in Sketch within a day.

After implementing the design system, it was much easier to create new pages and it became a lot more organized in finding files and organizing layers. Not only was Sketch more useful with the ‘Symbols' function, the files were also light-weight and offered many great plugins to improve my workflow to suit the lean / agile process.

Prototyping on Invision / Reiteration Cycles

Using the CRAFT plugin by Sketch, I was able to conveniently upload all artboards over to InVision within the app as opposed to manually adding the screens on the web app.

I organized my artboards into collections with a standardized naming convention.

InVision allows hotlinking of the artboards to make the prototype feel like a real app connecting pages.

Prior to InVision and before the expansion project, I developed a few pages initially that were minimal as it did not require much reiteration. I would present screens and ask for feedback through Google Docs, email, or as an attachment on Slack. As a result, getting feedback for content was slow and inefficient as there was miscommunication and confusion in where the feedback was pointing at. Furthermore, I was putting on 2 hats (designer and frontend developer) at the same time.

The scale of this project was different — Having to design and develop many more pages, go through vigorous code reviews, and testing for production-ready pages would take up a huge amount of time — I revisited my process and had to find a more efficient way to gather feedback from the business team. I gained inspiration from designers I networked with, learned best practices from reading articles on Medium, and picked up tips and valuable advice from communities such as UX Designer Community, Product Tribe, and Designer Hangouts and their approach working with a team. With my learnings, I minced the process into smaller digestable chunks — 1) UX Research 2) Prototype and Design 3) Reiteration Loop 4) Develop. I introduced InVision, a powerful prototyping tool, to the business team as a platform to provide feedback.

Once the screens were all linked and functioned just like an actual website, I would share the ready prototype to the business team. Feedback on specific areas of the page are demarcated clearer which resulted in faster iteration cycles and more efficient compared to the previous method.

Development

Once the design and content was completed, the next step was to 'change hats’ (web designer). I would then gather all assets from the high-fidelity mocks — processed images, custom CSS styles, icons etc. With everything I need for the website in hand, I go on to make the ‘modular pieces’ and ‘templates’ (idea from Brad Frost's Atomic Design Theory) which consists of 'modules' thanks to Jekyll, a Ruby-based static page generator, that makes use of tags in Front Matter and custom variables (Liquid) which I had implemented on the website modular (easier to change content like the header and footer across the entire website).

After the pages are coded out, I would make sure that the page is responsive for mobile, and optimized for search engines (Search Engine Optimization) by looking at meta descriptions and keywords before proceeding to test for deployment on a staging server before releasing for production.

Results and Success

We were able to produce the a few set of pages — ‘Solutions’, ‘IOTile', and a new front page and have been working on the next batch of webpages or improving all aspects. There were immediate results since the release of the ’Solution' pages on Google Analytics since August where these new pages account for 20.67% of the total visits since the release. The ‘IOTile / Product’ pages account for 58.92% of total views at the end of the week of release.
