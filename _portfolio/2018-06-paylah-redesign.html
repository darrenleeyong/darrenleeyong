Problem



Despite the new look on the app from an update this year, the peer-to-peer transfer process still felt sluggish and the app is cluttered with a surplus of underutilized features that potentially impedes frequent use. I felt that the user experience had so much room for improvement.

Research: a closer look at the¬†app

User Research/Problem Validation

"Was I the only one who felt that way?" I had to make sure. I did a guerrilla user research study in 2 weeks and interviewed 30+ participants of different demographics including working adults and a former employee of DBS, but majority were millennials in their 20's, and some of whom who had experience using other payment apps in countries such as the US and China. Some interviewees were unqualified as they were not PayLah! users but I still gathered insights of why they are not using the app.

Here are my findings:

  • 'Pay' and 'Scan QR' are the most-used functions in the app (No one interviewed used 'Request' or 'My QR code'.)
  • Most who use 'Scan QR' function started using it only during a campaign that gave a $0.50 discount (economic benefit) in select locations
  • 100% don't use/didn't noticed the everyday payments section on front page
  • 91% don't see/didn't notice the deals on the front page
  • Most don't know about the availability of PayNow function (a method to transfer inter-bank) inside PayLah! Few who knows about it said that it is not obvious
  • Non-users are not using PayLah! due to various reasons like confusion (e.g. PayNow versus PayLah!), difficulty in tracking expenses, security concerns, and the idea of handling yet another 'separate' account

An interesting finding from an ex-DBS employee was that banks like DBS are very aggressive in pushing for cashless to reduce ATM transactions. The cost of maintaining ATMs is very high due to the need to purchase security, transportation, and insurance for the machines implemented islandwide. Therefore, going cashless is a win-win for both parties‚Ää-‚Ääsaving costs for banks and providing convenience for users.

Competitive Analysis of DBS¬†PayLah!

To better assess the depth of the problem, I conducted a competitive analysis with similar services. When I was in the Bay Area in 2017, I was introduced to Venmo, a digital wallet app acquired by PayPal, to do transactions with friends. I had a great experience with the app and will be using Venmo as a comparison/benchmark. No wonder it was rated No. 1 money transfer app of 2018 by TechRadar as well as obtaining a revered status: verb (e.g "I just Venmoed you 5 bucks").

Seamless, frictionless peer-to-peer transfers

Making payments to my friends has never been so simple with Venmo. Venmo has a seamless transfer process where it takes only 8 steps (taps & events) to complete a transaction to a contact compared to DBS requiring at least 13 steps.



In Venmo, payments are people-centric‚Ää-‚Ääthe form has the recipient's field placed first, followed by the amount, and a compulsory description of the payment. When the 'pay/request' button is pressed, a contact list immediately pops up to your top people (people whom you had often transactions with) and friends list, skipping an extra tap. The process is also hastened by considerations such as a confirmation prompt on the same button and bringing the user directly to the home page displaying their successful transaction.

Home Page/Social Feed

According to Hick's Law, the more choices the users are presented with, the longer it takes to make a decision. The home page of PayLah! has 14 different buttons/choices. Including the little padding and lack of distinction between each feature, the home screen looks like a deposit of icons. Everything below the pink-gradient fold with the main features is more likely to be ignored as observed in my user research. Additionally, the lack of DBS's brand identity such as the deep red color or a missing logo does not provide a clue that the app was developed by DBS.

In Venmo, the first page you land shows a social feed of transactions by your friends. There is only one button on the top right to make/request a payment. It is straightforward and the user performs what they intend to do: monetary transfers.



"I use PayLah! for one purpose‚Ää-‚Ääto transfer to friends. Everything else, I don't really care."‚Ää-‚ÄäEntrepreneur, Male, mid-20's

Product Strategy

Venmo has taken on more of a social approach to peer-to-peer payments with features such as a social feed, forced messages within payments, encouraged use of emojis, and a global feed. It can be integrated with your Facebook account and the ease of seeing your friends (and their friends)allows one to make payments to mutual friends or people not in your contact list easily.



The approach DBS is taking is similar to WeChat Pay where payments can be made for anything, ranging from utility bills to movie tickets. Similar features are accessible on the PayLah home page but two problems exist: 1) it feels really cluttered and 2) no one I interviewed uses these functions.



"I like WeChat. Feels like PayLah! is copying. Can pay for movie tickets, bills, takeout, and a lot of things. I am impressed because can perform so many functions and doesn't feel confusing. It's very straightforward. But DBS PayLah! feels very messy and a bit hard to navigate. WeChat is clean and slick."‚Ää-‚Ää Graduate, Male, mid-20's

The Redesign

Users' Problems to¬†Solve

Based on my interview findings and research, these are the objectives identified for the redesign:

  • Increase visibility of PayNow service within app
  • Remove clutter in front page and improve information architecture within app
  • Make peer-to-peer payments fast, accounting for reach accessibility of one hand (think payments at store when other hand is occupied)
  • Make payment with QR code easier to use
  • Make finding unlisted people easier
  • Introduce economic benefit for increased usage
  • Last but not least, make the entire experience enjoyable



Wireframe and User Flow¬†Chart

Understanding the users' problems to be solved, I sketched out on pen and paper various versions of mock ups, with each screen aiming to solve the various problems users listed.
